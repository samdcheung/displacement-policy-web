Platform

What is Displacement Policy?

Displacement Policy is an independent research and policy platform examining how responsibility for displaced populations is assigned, financed, and sustained across public systems.

Its focus is not only on displacement itself, but on the institutional arrangements through which protection, services, and longer-term inclusion are governed: laws, administrative mandates, public finance, welfare systems, infrastructure, identity systems, and intergovernmental responsibility.

The platform connects fields that are often treated separately, including refugee protection, internal displacement, migration, humanitarian response, disaster risk management, development finance, social protection, and climate adaptation.

By bringing these fields together, Displacement Policy examines where public systems stop carrying responsibility, why institutional discontinuities persist, and how governance arrangements can be redesigned to follow people through displacement.

Research Agenda

A research agenda on displacement governance and public systems

Displacement governance often fails not because institutions are absent, but because existing systems are designed around fixed legal categories, territorial boundaries, sectoral mandates, and short budget cycles.

Displacement Policy examines how these arrangements produce gaps in responsibility as people move across jurisdictions, legal statuses, service systems, and phases of vulnerability.

The research is organised around three connected analytical lenses:

The displacement continuum

Identifies where responsibility becomes fragmented as people move across institutions, territories, and time.

Sustainable inclusion

Examines the conditions under which ordinary public systems can maintain responsibility for displaced populations.

Political economy

Explains how mandates, fiscal incentives, administrative rules, and institutional interests shape inclusion, exclusion, and continuity.

The agenda applies these lenses to social protection, public finance, climate mobility, infrastructure, identity systems, subnational governance, and other areas in which displacement intersects with ordinary state functions.

Founder & Director

The platform is rooted in operational experience across refugee protection, internal displacement, humanitarian coordination, public policy, and finance — and in the institutional questions that those fields often leave unresolved.

Displacement Policy was established by Samuel Cheung, a displacement and protection specialist with more than two decades of experience across humanitarian operations, public policy, and international institutions. Samuel served with UNHCR in field and headquarters roles across Asia, the Middle East, and Africa, including as Chief of Internal Displacement, Chief of Protection from Violence and Displacement, and Global Protection Cluster Coordinator.

He led or authored major international policy frameworks on protection leadership, internal displacement, and humanitarian protection coordination, and worked extensively on refugee protection, internal displacement, mixed migration, durable solutions, and humanitarian response. Prior to UNHCR, he advised Asian sovereigns on bond programmes and structured finance at Allen & Overy LLP in Hong Kong. He holds a Juris Doctor from Georgetown University and a Bachelor of Arts in Public Policy from Duke University.

These experiences inform the platform’s central questions: where institutional responsibility breaks down, how public systems can maintain continuity through displacement, and how those responsibilities can be financed and sustained through ordinary rather than exceptional arrangements.

Looking Forward

Displacement is no longer adequately understood as an exceptional condition requiring temporary response. For many states, it has become a recurrent feature of public administration, territorial governance, and public finance.

The resulting questions are not only humanitarian. They concern who carries responsibility when people move across jurisdictions, how public systems maintain access when legal or administrative status changes, and how governments finance obligations that extend beyond emergency timelines.

Displacement Policy exists to develop practical and analytically rigorous answers to those questions — and to translate them into research, policy propositions, governance tools, and institutional reform.
