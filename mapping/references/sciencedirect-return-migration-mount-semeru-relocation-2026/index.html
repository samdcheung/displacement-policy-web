Indonesia Post-Disaster Planned Relocation Programmes (Palu and Mount Semeru)

Following the September 2018 Palu earthquake, tsunami, and liquefaction disaster, which displaced roughly 170,000 people, the Indonesian government relocated communities from high-risk liquefaction and tsunami zones to new permanent housing, establishing a dedicated land task force to resolve overlapping claims on relocation sites. A comparable programme followed the December 2021 Mount Semeru eruption, which displaced nearly 10,000 people; the government's flagship Bumi Semeru Damai relocation site in Lumajang Regency built 1,951 permanent housing units, of which 751 remained unoccupied as of January 2025 according to local government data, with a 2025 household survey of returnees citing housing dissatisfaction and place attachment as key drivers of return to origin areas.

Why it matters: Indonesia demonstrates real technical and financial capacity to build large-scale replacement housing quickly after disasters, which distinguishes it from Lao PDR or Cambodia, where construction financing itself is the binding constraint. The political economy archetype is infrastructure-complete, occupancy-incomplete resettlement: the recurring pattern across Palu and Semeru, physically completed housing with substantial unoccupied stock years later, shows that construction capacity does not by itself secure durable relocation outcomes when livelihood restoration, land tenure, and social and cultural attachment to the place of origin are not addressed with equal investment.
