Timor-Leste Land Law and Post-Crisis IDP Resettlement Architecture

Following the 2006 political-security crisis that displaced up to 150,000 people, Timor-Leste's government pursued a cash-for-return programme to close all 65 IDP camps by 2009 under the National Recovery Strategy 'Hamutuk Hari'i Futuru,' choosing to promote return ahead of resolving the underlying property disputes that had contributed to displacement. Those land questions remained legally unresolved for nearly two decades until the enactment in 2025 of a new Land Law (the Special Regime for the Ownership of Immovable Property), which the government has framed as foundational to investment and development.

Why it matters: Timor-Leste is a rare completed case of mass IDP return in Southeast Asia, and its sequencing choice is the analytical point. The political economy archetype is return without resolution: prioritising rapid camp closure over property adjudication produced a durable-looking outcome on displacement statistics while leaving land conflict as the residual, multi-decade governance problem. A September 2025 World Bank assessment found that, even with the new law's legal foundations in place, fragmented land administration continues to constrain implementation - the pattern seen across the dataset in which passing a law is necessary but not sufficient for institutional absorption.
