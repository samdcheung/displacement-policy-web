ANALYTICAL FRAMEWORK

How displacement governance breaks — and how institutions can sustain responsibility

A framework for analysing where institutional responsibility fragments, what continuity requires, and which forms of reform are politically and fiscally feasible.

A governance problem of institutional design

Displacement is commonly governed through legal categories, sectoral mandates, emergency procedures and project-based responses. Yet displacement itself is rarely contained by those boundaries. People move across legal statuses, administrative jurisdictions, public systems and phases of vulnerability, while the institutions responsible for protection, services and financing remain organised around fixed categories, mandates, territories and time horizons.

The resulting protection gaps are therefore not explained adequately by the absence of law, weak coordination or institutional incapacity alone. They often arise because otherwise functional governance systems are designed in ways that fragment responsibility precisely where displacement unsettles established boundaries of membership, mandate authority and fiscal exposure.

The analytical framework developed by Displacement Policy brings together three connected elements. The displacement continuum is a governance diagnostic that locates the points at which institutional responsibility breaks. Sustainable inclusion defines the institutional conditions required to maintain protection through ordinary public systems. Political economy explains why fragmentation persists, how institutional interests shape the terms of inclusion, and where reform may be feasible.

Together, these concepts shift the analytical focus from displacement as a sequence of exceptional categories toward the governance architecture through which responsibility is assigned, financed and sustained over time.

The Displacement Continuum

The displacement continuum begins from a mismatch between the lived reality of displacement and the institutional architecture through which states govern it. For displaced people, movement is often continuous: across borders, districts, legal categories, programmes and successive phases of vulnerability. For governance systems, movement is usually the exception. Eligibility, budgets and administrative responsibility are organised around bounded populations, defined jurisdictions and discrete events.

Applied as a governance diagnostic, the continuum identifies the points at which displaced people cross institutional thresholds but governance architecture does not follow. It therefore shifts analysis from legal status alone to institutional function: which system is responsible, under what mandate, for how long, with what budget authority, and what happens when a person crosses the boundary on which that responsibility depends?

Categorical fault lines

Categorical fault lines arise where legal or administrative classifications determine eligibility and responsibility more rigidly than displacement-related vulnerability. A person may move from recognised refugee to irregular migrant across a border, from disaster-affected household to ordinary social-assistance applicant after an emergency declaration expires, or from project-affected person to resident once a resettlement programme closes. The category changes even when the underlying need for protection or public support does not.

Temporal fault lines

Temporal fault lines arise from the mismatch between time-bounded institutional responses and the longitudinal character of displacement. Emergency powers expire, budget authority reverts, programme eligibility requires requalification and project obligations close, while displacement and its social and economic effects continue. What appears operationally as a transition from response to recovery can therefore become an institutional drop-off in responsibility.

Territorial fault lines

Territorial fault lines arise because governance and public finance are organised territorially while displaced populations move. Registration, service access, social protection eligibility and fiscal transfers are often tied to fixed jurisdictions. When people cross those boundaries, receiving authorities may absorb new costs without corresponding transfers, while administrative records, entitlements and responsibility remain anchored elsewhere.

Where does the system stop following the person?

The analytical contribution of the continuum is to make fragmentation specific and observable. It directs attention to the mechanisms through which discontinuity is produced: eligibility rules, mandate boundaries, budget cycles, registries, territorial financing arrangements and project timelines. Fragmentation is therefore treated not as an abstract coordination problem, but as a predictable outcome of institutional design.

Sustainable Inclusion

If the displacement continuum identifies where responsibility breaks down, sustainable inclusion specifies the governance response. It asks whether displacement-related protection can be embedded within the ordinary institutions through which states already govern health, education, social assistance, civil registration, local services and public finance.

Sustainable inclusion is not equivalent to formal legal integration, nor does it require identical status or entitlement across all displaced populations. Its concern is institutional continuity: whether people can remain connected to essential systems as their legal position, location or phase of displacement changes, without depending indefinitely on parallel humanitarian structures or discretionary exceptions.

Three institutional conditions are central.

Systems integration

Systems integration concerns the alignment of eligibility rules, administrative procedures and service-delivery pathways so that displaced populations can access existing systems rather than remain confined to parallel caseloads. Its test is not simply whether access is formally permitted, but whether records, eligibility and service relationships remain functional as circumstances change.

Fiscal integration

Fiscal integration concerns the incorporation of displacement-related responsibilities into ordinary budget and financing arrangements. Temporary emergency allocations may meet immediate needs, but durable inclusion requires some capacity to convert exceptional expenditure into predictable public responsibility without creating politically or fiscally unsustainable commitments. This makes financing architecture part of governance design rather than a separate implementation question.

Institutional responsibility

Institutional responsibility concerns mandates that persist across phases of displacement and administrative boundaries. Responsibility must be anchored in authority, accountability and budget capacity, rather than relying solely on voluntary coordination between institutions whose formal mandates remain unchanged.

Can public systems maintain responsibility as displacement evolves?

Where sustainable inclusion has advanced, it has often done so not by overcoming institutional interests but by working through them: where inclusion reinforces a sectoral mandate, reduces the costs of administrative exclusion, or can be structured in fiscally bounded and politically acceptable ways. The practical question is therefore not only whether inclusion is normatively desirable, but under what institutional configuration it can be sustained.

Political Economy

The persistence of fragmentation within administratively capable states requires an explanation beyond coordination failure. Governance systems embody institutional interests: they define who belongs and on what terms, which institutions exercise authority, how mandates are protected and how fiscal exposure is bounded. Displacement unsettles each of these arrangements.

Membership regimes may rely on temporary or reversible categories that preserve sovereign discretion. Ministries may resist responsibilities that expand their mandate without corresponding authority or resources. Budget systems may prefer temporary and externally financed expenditure to recurrent obligations that become part of the fiscal baseline. Parallel arrangements can therefore persist not because institutions are dysfunctional, but because they allow states and agencies to extend limited protection without altering the underlying distribution of authority, membership or fiscal responsibility.

Political economy also explains why inclusion sometimes becomes possible. Reform can emerge where sectoral objectives align with inclusion, where the administrative or fiscal costs of exclusion become greater than the costs of absorption, or where conditional forms of access can be introduced without requiring broader legal or political incorporation.

Accordingly, reform is not simply a matter of identifying an institutional gap and designing a technically superior solution. It requires understanding the incentives that reproduce the gap and the political, fiscal and administrative conditions under which those incentives can be realigned.

How the Framework Works

The three elements perform distinct but connected analytical functions.

The displacement continuum

Diagnoses where responsibility fragments across categorical, temporal and territorial boundaries, and identifies the institutional mechanisms producing those discontinuities.

Sustainable inclusion

Defines the institutional conditions required to maintain continuity through systems integration, fiscal integration and durable assignment of responsibility.

Political economy

Explains why fragmentation persists, which interests and incentives sustain it, and what forms of institutional change may be feasible.

Used together, the framework moves analysis from the existence of a protection gap to its institutional cause and then to the conditions under which that gap could realistically be closed.

It can be applied across refugee situations, internal displacement, disaster and climate mobility, development-induced displacement, social protection, infrastructure, public finance and other contexts in which people move across institutional boundaries while responsibility remains fixed.

How the Framework Is Used

The framework can be applied to a country, public system, displacement context or financing architecture.

  1. Identify the transition points

    Where do people move across legal categories, jurisdictions, programmes, institutions or phases of displacement?

  2. Locate the discontinuity

    Where do responsibility, eligibility, data, services, mandates or financing cease to follow?

  3. Identify the institutional mechanism

    Which rules, budget arrangements, mandate boundaries, registries, project structures or jurisdictional divisions produce the discontinuity?

  4. Identify the pathway to continuity

    Which public system, institutional mandate, financing mechanism or administrative arrangement could sustain responsibility across the transition?

  5. Assess political economy and reform feasibility

    Which interests and incentives sustain the existing arrangement, and what changes could improve continuity while remaining administratively, fiscally and politically sustainable?
