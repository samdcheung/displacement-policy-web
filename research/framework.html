ANALYTICAL FRAMEWORK

Governing displacement across institutions, systems and time

The displacement continuum, sustainable inclusion and political economy form the analytical framework for Displacement Policy’s research agenda.

Framework overview

Displacement is experienced as a continuum, while governance is organised through fixed legal categories, territorial jurisdictions, institutional mandates and financing arrangements.

The result is often institutional discontinuity. Responsibility, eligibility, data, services and financing fail to move with people as they cross borders, jurisdictions, administrative categories and phases of displacement.

The analytical framework developed by Displacement Policy examines this gap between the longitudinal reality of displacement and the institutional architecture through which states respond to it.

It is organised around three core concepts:

The Displacement Continuum identifies where institutional responsibility breaks down.

Sustainable Inclusion examines what is required for public systems to maintain protection and responsibility over time.

Political Economy explains why fragmentation persists, where incentives for reform exist, and what forms of institutional change may be politically and fiscally feasible.

The framework links the Displacement Continuum, Sustainable Inclusion and Political Economy to examine how displacement is governed across institutions, systems and time.

This analytical framework is developed in greater depth in Samuel Cheung’s forthcoming chapter, “Bridging the Fault Lines: The Displacement Continuum and Sustainable Inclusion in Southeast Asia.”

The Displacement Continuum

The displacement continuum provides a governance diagnostic.

Displacement rarely occurs as a single event or within a stable legal category. People may move internally and across borders, between emergency and protracted situations, across administrative jurisdictions, and through changing legal and institutional classifications.

Yet governance systems are typically organised around fixed categories and bounded mandates.

The displacement continuum shifts attention from status alone towards the points where institutional responsibility breaks down. It asks whether protection, eligibility, data, services and financing continue as people move across:

  • legal and administrative categories;
  • territorial jurisdictions;
  • institutional mandates; and
  • phases and durations of displacement.

The central question is not only which legal or policy framework applies, but whether governance systems are capable of following people over time.

REFORM

Sustainable Inclusion

Sustainable inclusion describes the direction of reform.

It focuses on how displacement-related protection, risks and responsibilities can be progressively embedded within ordinary institutions, public systems and financing arrangements rather than remaining dependent on parallel, exceptional or temporary responses.

Achieving sustainable inclusion requires institutional adaptation: changes in mandates, eligibility rules, data systems, financing arrangements and administrative capacity so that public systems can function under conditions of mobility, changing jurisdictions and prolonged displacement.

This may involve stronger links with social protection, health, education, civil registration, local government, public finance and other systems that already sustain responsibility for populations over time.

Sustainable inclusion does not imply a single model of legal incorporation. Institutional arrangements must be politically feasible, fiscally sustainable and adapted to national context.

The central test is whether public systems can absorb displacement-related vulnerability while maintaining continuity of responsibility.

Political Economy

Institutional fragmentation is not always simply a coordination failure.

Existing arrangements may persist because they contain fiscal exposure, preserve institutional mandates, protect sovereign discretion or reflect wider political interests.

Political economy therefore explains why discontinuities persist, where incentives for reform exist, and what kinds of institutional change may be feasible.

Political economy also shapes whether institutional adaptation is incentivised, resisted or sustained over time.

It also connects displacement governance with questions of leadership: how decision-makers navigate competing mandates, political constraints and institutional incentives in order to sustain protection outcomes beyond temporary or humanitarian structures.

How the Framework Works Together

Political Economy

What reform is feasible, and why?

Political economy identifies the incentives, constraints and institutional interests that determine which forms of inclusion can be sustained.

Institutional adaptation is the process through which systems adjust between diagnosis and sustained inclusion — changing mandates, financing, data and administrative arrangements so that continuity can be maintained in practice.
