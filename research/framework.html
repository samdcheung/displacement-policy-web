ANALYTICAL FRAMEWORK

Governing displacement across institutions, systems and time

Displacement is experienced as a continuum, while governance is organised through fixed legal categories, territorial jurisdictions, institutional mandates and financing arrangements.

The result is often institutional discontinuity. Responsibility, eligibility, data, services and financing fail to move with people as they cross borders, jurisdictions, administrative categories and phases of displacement.

The analytical framework developed by Displacement Policy examines this gap between the longitudinal reality of displacement and the institutional architecture through which states respond to it.

It is organised around three core concepts:

The Displacement Continuum identifies where institutional responsibility breaks down.

Sustainable Inclusion examines what is required for public systems to maintain protection and responsibility over time.

Political Economy explains why fragmentation persists, where incentives for reform exist, and what forms of institutional change may be politically and fiscally feasible.

This analytical framework is developed in greater depth in Samuel Cheung’s forthcoming chapter, “Bridging the Fault Lines: The Displacement Continuum and Sustainable Inclusion in Southeast Asia.”

FRAMEWORK LOGIC

The Analytical Engine

DIAGNOSTIC

The Displacement Continuum

The displacement continuum provides a governance diagnostic.

Displacement rarely occurs as a single event or within a stable legal category. People may move internally and across borders, between emergency and protracted situations, across administrative jurisdictions, and through changing legal and institutional classifications.

Yet governance systems are typically organised around fixed categories and bounded mandates.

The displacement continuum shifts attention from status alone towards the points where institutional responsibility breaks down. It asks whether protection, eligibility, data, services and financing continue as people move across:

  • legal and administrative categories;
  • territorial jurisdictions;
  • institutional mandates; and
  • phases and durations of displacement.

The central question is not only which legal or policy framework applies, but whether governance systems are capable of following people over time.

DIRECTION OF REFORM

Sustainable Inclusion

Sustainable inclusion describes the direction of reform.

It focuses on how displacement-related protection, risks and responsibilities can be progressively embedded within ordinary institutions, public systems and financing arrangements rather than remaining dependent on parallel, exceptional or temporary responses.

Achieving sustainable inclusion requires institutional adaptation: changes in mandates, eligibility rules, data systems, financing arrangements and administrative capacity so that public systems can function under conditions of mobility, changing jurisdictions and prolonged displacement.

This may involve stronger links with social protection, health, education, civil registration, local government, public finance and other systems that already sustain responsibility for populations over time.

Sustainable inclusion does not imply a single model of legal incorporation. Institutional arrangements must be politically feasible, fiscally sustainable and adapted to national context.

The central test is whether public systems can absorb displacement-related vulnerability while maintaining continuity of responsibility.

PATHWAY OF CHANGE

Institutional Adaptation

Institutional adaptation concerns how public systems change in order to maintain continuity under displacement.

It may involve several forms of adaptation:

Scalability — the ability of systems to expand or adjust in response to shocks.

Portability and horizontal integration — the ability of eligibility, data, financing and services to move across jurisdictions and administrative categories.

Absorptive capacity — the ability of receiving institutions and local systems to incorporate population change without undermining service quality or creating unmanaged fiscal exposure.

Institutional adaptation is therefore not a separate end state. It is the process through which sustainable inclusion becomes operationally possible.

FEASIBILITY

Political Economy

Institutional fragmentation is not always simply a coordination failure.

Existing arrangements may persist because they contain fiscal exposure, preserve institutional mandates, protect sovereign discretion or reflect wider political interests.

Political economy therefore explains why discontinuities persist, where incentives for reform exist, and what kinds of institutional change may be feasible.

Political economy also shapes whether institutional adaptation is incentivised, resisted or sustained over time.

It also connects displacement governance with questions of leadership: how decision-makers navigate competing mandates, political constraints and institutional incentives in order to sustain protection outcomes beyond temporary or humanitarian structures.

From Framework to Research

Public Systems and Social Protection

This area examines whether ordinary public systems can respond not only to shocks, but to mobility.

Research focuses on how social protection, health, education, civil registration and other public systems can maintain access as people move across jurisdictions, administrative categories and phases of displacement.

Key questions include portability of benefits, eligibility across jurisdictions, interoperable registries, identification, duration-sensitive support and the relationship between humanitarian assistance and national systems.

The wider question is whether systems designed around stable populations and fixed administrative categories can maintain protection and access as people move.

Here, institutional adaptation includes portability, interoperable registries, eligibility reform and changes in administrative delivery so that systems can function under mobility.

Relevant to: social protection, humanitarian policy, health and education systems, civil registration, digital public infrastructure and public service delivery.

Public Finance and Displacement

This area examines how displacement-related responsibilities are recognised, allocated and financed through public and sovereign fiscal systems.

Research focuses on the fiscal architecture underlying displacement: national and subnational budgets, intergovernmental transfers, contingency financing, development finance and other mechanisms through which displacement-related costs and responsibilities are distributed and sustained over time.

A central challenge is the recurrent obligation problem: how to incorporate displacement-related expenditure into ordinary public systems without creating perceptions of unlimited long-term fiscal responsibility.

The research examines how fiscal systems reproduce categorical, temporal and territorial fault lines; how financing arrangements shape incentives for inclusion; and how displacement-related pressures intersect with sovereign resilience, development finance and emerging financing instruments.

Here, institutional adaptation concerns whether budgets, fiscal transfers and financing arrangements adjust as displacement-related responsibilities become recurrent or move across jurisdictions.

Relevant to: public finance, fiscal policy, development finance, sovereign finance, intergovernmental fiscal systems, multilateral development banks and humanitarian financing.

Development, Infrastructure and Displacement

Development investments can generate displacement while also shaping the institutions that respond to it.

This area examines how infrastructure finance, environmental and social safeguards and development-bank operations connect project-based displacement responses with longer-term public responsibility.

The focus extends beyond compensation and project compliance to the institutions that sustain responsibility after project closure: public services, social protection, local government, financing and continuing support for affected populations.

A central question is what happens when project obligations end but displacement-related vulnerability continues.

Relevant to: multilateral development banks, infrastructure finance, environmental and social safeguards, development-induced displacement, public investment and country programming.

Climate, Disasters and Mobility

Climate change and disasters increasingly expose the limitations of systems designed around temporary shocks and fixed populations.

This area examines how adaptive social protection, disaster risk management, anticipatory action, planned mobility and resilience financing can function across the displacement continuum.

The focus is on institutional continuity before, during and after movement — and on how adaptation and resilience systems can reduce the risk that mobility itself creates new forms of exclusion.

Research considers how responsibility, eligibility and financing change as mobility moves across emergency, recovery, relocation and longer-term inclusion.

Here, institutional adaptation includes the capacity of systems to move beyond shock response towards portability, receiving-area capacity and longer-term absorption.

Relevant to: climate mobility, disaster risk reduction, adaptation, resilience, anticipatory action, planned relocation and loss and damage.

Legal Status, Territory and Multilevel Governance

Displacement often crosses the boundaries through which responsibility is administratively organised.

This area examines what happens when people move between legal statuses, local jurisdictions, national systems and cross-border frameworks.

Research focuses on responsibility allocation, transfers of responsibility across institutions and jurisdictions, territorial governance, local government, regional cooperation and the interaction between legal protection and administrative systems.

It also examines where sector-specific or administrative forms of inclusion may be possible where comprehensive legal status reform is politically constrained.

Relevant to: refugee and IDP protection, migration governance, statelessness, local government, regional institutions, legal policy and public administration.

Institutional adaptation and absorption architecture

The concept is particularly important in research on climate mobility and planned movement.

Legal pathways or mobility options alone are insufficient if receiving systems cannot absorb additional populations.

Institutional adaptation therefore includes the administrative, fiscal and service-delivery changes needed to support absorption: portable eligibility, interoperable data, receiving-area fiscal transfers, local service capacity and financing arrangements that can sustain inclusion over time.

This logic underpins emerging work on absorption architecture in climate mobility, including the ADBI-MPI research proposal.

Southeast Asia Governance Mapping

Displacement Policy’s research agenda is global in scope. Southeast Asia provides its initial regional evidence base and a comparative setting in which to test broader questions about displacement governance.

The Southeast Asia Governance Mapping examines how countries across the region organise responsibility for displacement through laws, institutions, public systems and policy instruments.

Southeast Asia provides a particularly valuable setting for this work. States combine significant administrative capacity and increasingly sophisticated systems for social protection, disaster management, migration governance and public service delivery with diverse legal and political approaches to refugees, internal displacement, statelessness, disasters and mobility.

The mapping identifies where responsibility is assigned, where it becomes fragmented, and how governance systems respond as people move across legal categories, territorial jurisdictions and phases of displacement.

Its purpose is not simply to catalogue policies. It provides an empirical base for comparative research on institutional continuity, sustainable inclusion, public finance and political economy, while generating propositions that can be tested and developed in other regions.
