ANALYTICAL FRAMEWORK

How displacement governance breaks — and how institutions can sustain responsibility

Introduction

Displacement Policy examines displacement as a problem of institutional design: where responsibility breaks down as people move across legal categories, administrative jurisdictions, public systems and time.

The framework combines three connected elements. The displacement continuum identifies where governance systems fail to follow people. Sustainable inclusion defines the institutional conditions required to maintain continuity of responsibility. Political economy explains why fragmentation persists, where incentives for reform exist, and what forms of institutional change may be feasible.

Together, these concepts shift attention from displacement as a series of exceptional categories toward the governance systems through which responsibility is assigned, financed and sustained.

The Displacement Continuum

Displacement is lived as a continuous condition, but governance systems are usually organised around fixed categories, jurisdictions, mandates and timeframes.

The displacement continuum is a governance diagnostic for identifying the points at which institutional responsibility becomes fragmented. It focuses on three recurring fault lines:

Categorical fault lines

Categorical fault lines arise when eligibility and responsibility depend on fixed legal or administrative status. A person may move from refugee to irregular migrant, from disaster-affected household to ordinary social-assistance applicant, or from project-affected person to resident of a new jurisdiction without continuity of protection.

Temporal fault lines

Temporal fault lines arise when institutional responses are designed to expire before displacement-related vulnerability has ended. Emergency declarations close, project cycles finish, budget authority lapses and temporary programmes terminate while displacement persists.

Territorial fault lines

Territorial fault lines arise when people move across administrative or national boundaries but responsibility, data, services and financing remain tied to the jurisdiction they have left.

The central question is simple:

Where does the system stop following the person?

The continuum therefore shifts analysis away from legal status alone and towards the institutional pathways through which responsibility is maintained — or lost — across displacement.

Sustainable Inclusion

If the displacement continuum identifies where responsibility breaks down, sustainable inclusion describes what institutional continuity would require.

Sustainable inclusion means embedding displacement-related protection and vulnerability within ordinary public systems rather than relying indefinitely on parallel, exceptional or temporary arrangements.

It depends on three institutional conditions:

Systems integration

Displaced populations can access ordinary public systems through eligibility rules, administrative procedures and service-delivery pathways that do not collapse when status or location changes.

Fiscal integration

Displacement-related responsibilities are progressively reflected in regular budgets and financing arrangements rather than depending solely on temporary humanitarian or emergency funding.

Institutional responsibility

Mandates and accountability remain clear across agencies, levels of government and phases of displacement, so responsibility does not disappear as circumstances change.

Sustainable inclusion does not require identical legal status or uniform entitlements across all populations. It asks a more practical governance question:

Can public systems maintain responsibility as displacement evolves?

Political Economy

Institutional fragmentation is not simply a coordination failure.

Governance systems reflect underlying political and institutional interests: who belongs and on what terms, which institution holds authority, how mandates are protected, and how much fiscal exposure the state is prepared to absorb.

These incentives shape both exclusion and reform.

Membership systems may preserve sovereign discretion by maintaining reversible or temporary categories. Sectoral mandates may resist responsibilities that fall outside established institutional boundaries. Budget systems may favour bounded and temporary expenditure over open-ended commitments.

Political economy therefore asks why discontinuities persist even where administrative capacity exists, and under what conditions institutions may have incentives to change.

The central question is:

What would make continuity institutionally and politically feasible?

How the Framework Works

The three elements perform different analytical functions.

The displacement continuum identifies where responsibility breaks.

Sustainable inclusion specifies what institutional continuity would require.

Political economy explains why fragmentation persists and what forms of reform may be feasible.

Used together, they provide a way to analyse displacement not only as a protection or legal issue, but as a question of governance architecture.

The framework can be applied across refugee situations, internal displacement, disaster and climate mobility, development-induced displacement, social protection, infrastructure, public finance and other areas where people move across institutional boundaries.

How the Framework Is Used

The framework can be applied to a country, public system, displacement context or financing architecture.

  1. Identify the transition points

    Where do people move across legal categories, jurisdictions, programmes, institutions or phases of displacement?

  2. Locate the discontinuity

    Where do responsibility, eligibility, data, services or financing break down?

  3. Identify the institutional pathway

    Which existing public system, mandate, financing mechanism or administrative arrangement could maintain continuity?

  4. Examine the political economy

    What institutional interests, fiscal incentives, sovereignty concerns or mandate constraints sustain the existing arrangement?

  5. Assess reform feasibility

    What changes could improve continuity while remaining administratively, fiscally and politically sustainable?
