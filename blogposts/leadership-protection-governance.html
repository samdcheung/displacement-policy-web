Leadership, Protection, and Governance

Why protection outcomes often reflect institutional incentives and leadership choices more than policy commitments.

Samuel Cheung · June 2026 · Governance · 4–5 min read

This reflection draws in part on the author's experience leading development of UNHCR's Protection Leadership framework while serving in the Division of International Protection.

The gap between protection policy and protection outcome is one of the most documented and least resolved problems in displacement governance. International frameworks are more developed than at any point in the history of the regime. Coordination architecture has been refined through successive reform cycles. Agencies with explicit protection mandates are present in nearly every major displacement context. Yet outcomes for displaced populations often remain strikingly similar across decades of normative progress.

The standard explanations locate the problem in policy: insufficient legal frameworks, weak implementation, inadequate political will, or failures of coordination. These explanations are not wrong, but they are incomplete. What they often miss is that exclusion and fragmentation are not always failures of policy commitment. They are frequently the predictable outputs of institutional systems operating according to their underlying incentives and constraints.

Fiscal architectures are designed to limit long-term liabilities. Eligibility systems are built around stable categories of membership. Administrative structures distribute responsibility across ministries and agencies with different mandates and priorities. Displacement unsettles each of these arrangements. As a result, populations experiencing displacement often find themselves moving between systems rather than being absorbed by them.

Protection leadership matters within this landscape—more than it is sometimes credited, and more than the structural argument that follows might imply. Sustained analysis, principled advocacy with governments and duty-bearers, and coordination that builds genuine coalitions rather than simply convening actors are not peripheral to protection outcomes. They shape what is possible within constrained institutional environments.

A representative who builds trust with a finance ministry over years creates openings that legal frameworks alone cannot. An agency that invests in protection analysis—moving beyond operational reporting to diagnose the political, fiscal, and institutional dynamics driving risk—provides something that cannot easily be substituted. Leadership choices accumulate into institutional cultures, and institutional cultures shape outcomes at scale.

But leadership operates within structural conditions it cannot itself change.

Across Southeast Asia and beyond, many middle-income states possess sophisticated social protection systems, functioning disaster governance institutions, and growing administrative capacity. Yet displaced populations frequently remain excluded from these systems. The reason is often less about leadership quality than about institutional design.

Budget systems are organized around bounded commitments and annual expenditure cycles. Eligibility frameworks are designed for recognized categories of citizens, workers, or residents. Social programmes are typically developed to address predictable forms of vulnerability rather than populations whose status is uncertain, mobile, or politically contested. These features are not accidental. They reflect deliberate governance choices. They also create persistent disincentives against the kind of institutional inclusion that durable protection requires.

This is where the discussion becomes particularly relevant for international financial institutions and development banks.

Much of the displacement debate continues to be framed through humanitarian lenses. Yet many of the constraints determining whether displaced populations gain access to services, social protection, and public systems are embedded within fiscal and institutional structures that lie beyond the reach of humanitarian actors. Development finance institutions engage these systems directly through country diagnostics, fiscal assessments, public sector reform programmes, social protection investments, and infrastructure financing.

The critical question is whether displacement is treated primarily as a humanitarian problem requiring temporary management, or as a governance condition requiring institutional adaptation. The difference is consequential. One approach focuses on mitigating risks generated by displacement. The other focuses on redesigning systems so that they are capable of absorbing displacement as a recurrent feature of contemporary governance.

The distinction matters because leadership without structural change produces a familiar pattern. Access improves. Coordination strengthens. Analysis becomes more sophisticated. Individual programmes succeed. Yet the same populations often remain excluded from the same public systems during the next crisis cycle because the institutional architecture that produced exclusion has not fundamentally changed.

Policy commitments establish the normative horizon. Leadership determines how far responses move toward that horizon. But the distance between commitment and outcome is often determined by institutional design.

Improving protection outcomes therefore requires more than stronger policy frameworks and better leadership. It requires attention to the fiscal systems, eligibility structures, administrative arrangements, and governance incentives that determine whether inclusion is institutionally possible. Until those underlying structures are addressed, the gap between protection commitments and protection outcomes is likely to remain one of the defining challenges of displacement governance.
