Research

Understanding Displacement Governance

Displacement is often approached as a humanitarian challenge. This research programme examines displacement as a governance challenge: how responsibility is organised, financed, and sustained across institutions over time.

The research draws on governance, public administration, social protection, public finance, and development policy to examine how states respond when displacement becomes a recurrent public obligation rather than a temporary emergency.

Core Concepts

Frameworks for continuity, finance, absorption, and inclusion.

Framework

The Displacement Continuum

Displacement is rarely a single event. People move across legal categories, institutions, territories, and time horizons. Yet governance systems are often organised around static mandates and administrative boundaries.

The Displacement Continuum provides a framework for identifying where responsibility breaks down, where institutions stop following people, and where protection and assistance become fragmented.

The framework shifts attention from displacement categories toward continuity of governance responsibility.

Framework

Fiscal Architecture of Displacement

Displacement creates long-term obligations that must ultimately be financed through public systems.

This framework examines how budgets, social protection programmes, public expenditure systems, and development finance shape responses to displacement. It asks how states absorb recurrent costs and what sustainable financing arrangements require.

The objective is to move beyond funding appeals and examine displacement as a question of fiscal governance.

Concept

Institutional Absorption Capacity

Displacement places pressure on institutions responsible for services, protection, livelihoods, and local administration.

This research examines the capacity of institutions and public systems to absorb displaced populations through existing governance arrangements rather than parallel structures.

The focus is not only whether assistance exists, but whether ordinary systems can carry displacement-related vulnerability over time.

Concept

Sustainable Inclusion

Long-term solutions depend on whether displaced populations can access services, livelihoods, rights, and opportunities through ordinary public systems.

This research explores how inclusion is achieved, where barriers persist, and how governance arrangements influence long-term outcomes.

From Framework to Application

The concepts developed through this research are applied through Displacement Governance Mapping.

The current phase focuses on Southeast Asia as the first regional application of the methodology.
