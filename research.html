Research

Displacement governance and public systems

Why capable states still fail displaced populations — and what institutional design can do about it.

This research examines how responsibility for displaced populations is organised, financed, and sustained through ordinary systems of governance — and why protection gaps often persist even where states have sophisticated administrative, fiscal, and service-delivery capacity.

The Displacement Continuum

Three fault lines in institutional design.

Categorical

Status without continuity

Eligibility systems organised around categories such as citizen, worker, irregular migrant, refugee, or disaster-affected person often reset responsibility even when vulnerability continues.

Temporal

Emergency design for chronic reality

Budget authority is annual, emergency declarations expire, and programme eligibility requires requalification, even when displacement becomes prolonged.

Territorial

Jurisdictions that do not follow people

Social registries, civil registration, and service delivery systems are administered by fixed units, while displaced populations move across administrative boundaries.

Named Findings

Concepts developed through the research programme.

Brief 1 / Chapter

Paradox of Capacity

States with sophisticated administrative systems may still produce fragmented outcomes because institutions are designed around fixed categories and bounded mandates.

Brief 2

Infrastructure Finance Gap

Development-induced displacement often extends beyond project safeguards, leaving affected populations governed at the point of displacement but institutionally orphaned afterward.

Brief 3

Precision Exclusion Problem

Adaptive social protection systems are increasingly precise; when they exclude forcibly displaced people, exclusion may be architectural rather than accidental.

Brief 4

Recurrent Fiscal Obligation Problem

Sovereign fiscal systems resist treating displacement-related spending as a recurrent obligation, even when displacement creates long-term pressure on public systems.

Brief 5

Displacement as Sovereign Risk

Displacement-responsive systems can reduce future instability and emergency expenditure, but are often treated as fiscal liabilities rather than resilience investments.
