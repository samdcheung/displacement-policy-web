Research

Displacement governance and public systems

Why capable states still fail displaced populations — and what institutional design can do about it.

Displacement is often treated as a humanitarian challenge. Yet for most displaced populations, access to protection, services, and solutions depends not on humanitarian actors alone, but on the public systems that govern territory, welfare, infrastructure, and public finance.

This research examines how those systems are designed, how they fail, and under what conditions they can be reformed. Its focus is not only the movement of people, but the institutional arrangements through which responsibility is assigned, financed, and sustained over time.

Framework I

The Displacement Continuum

Displacement is rarely a single event. People move across legal categories, administrative jurisdictions, and phases of vulnerability while governance systems remain organised around fixed mandates, bounded eligibility rules, and territorial authority.

The Displacement Continuum is a diagnostic for locating precisely where institutional responsibility dissolves.

Fault Line I

Categorical

Governance systems allocate rights and responsibilities through categories — refugee, internally displaced person, disaster-affected household, project-affected community — designed to be bounded and exclusive.

The protection gap is produced by categorical design, not incidental to it.

Fault Line II

Temporal

Emergency declarations expire. Budget cycles reset. Eligibility requires requalification. Project obligations close on schedule. Displacement frequently does not resolve on any of these timelines.

The architecture is designed to prevent exceptional expenditure from becoming permanent obligation.

Fault Line III

Territorial

Public systems are organised around fixed administrative units. Displaced populations move across districts, provinces, borders, and jurisdictions while responsibility remains anchored to the territory where it was assigned.

The result is a recurring disconnection between population movement and administrative responsibility.

Framework II

Sustainable Inclusion

If the Displacement Continuum identifies where governance fails, Sustainable Inclusion defines the conditions under which governance can endure.

The concept shifts attention from exceptional programmes and temporary responses to the capacity of ordinary public systems to absorb displacement-related vulnerability over time. Displacement governance in Southeast Asia shows that inclusion has advanced not simply through normative commitment, but where institutional interests could be accommodated.

Thailand’s migrant health insurance scheme extended substantial coverage not through protection logic alone, but through public health and cost-recovery imperatives. The Philippines embedded disaster response within social protection through administrative arrangements that avoided amending statutory eligibility. Indonesia framed social protection expansion around nutrition and welfare mandates rather than displacement as such.

What these cases share is alignment between inclusion and institutional interests. Sustainable inclusion is therefore not principally a legal outcome. It is an institutional one. Protection becomes durable where it is anchored within the fiscal and administrative logics that states can sustain.

Dimension I

Systems Integration

Displaced populations access existing public systems — health, education, civil registration, social protection — rather than parallel humanitarian delivery mechanisms established specifically for displacement contexts.

Dimension II

Fiscal Integration

Displacement-related expenditure is incorporated within ordinary public finance arrangements rather than funded exclusively through temporary humanitarian or project-based mechanisms.

Dimension III

Institutional Responsibility

Responsibility is anchored within mandates, legislation, and administrative authority rather than dependent on discretionary coordination or exceptional arrangements that expire with the crisis that produced them.

Framework III

Fiscal Architecture of Displacement

The long-term capacity of states to absorb displacement is shaped as much by public finance as by law or policy.

Humanitarian financing matters. But the systems that ultimately determine absorption capacity are sovereign budgets, fiscal rules, intergovernmental transfers, infrastructure investment, and sovereign debt frameworks.

In a global sovereign bond market exceeding US$65 trillion, even marginal shifts in sovereign borrowing costs can outweigh entire annual humanitarian appeals. Middle-income states in Southeast Asia operate within sovereign financing environments shaped by credit ratings, debt sustainability assessments, and investor expectations regarding long-term fiscal exposure.

This is the recurrent fiscal obligation problem: the structural difficulty of incorporating displacement-related expenditure into ordinary state budgets without creating perceptions of unlimited long-term responsibility. It shapes, more than any coordination failure, why capable states maintain fragmented and temporary arrangements.

How can displacement be incorporated into ordinary state budgets without creating perceptions of unlimited long-term fiscal responsibility?

The Structural Argument

Why Fragmentation Persists

Conventional explanations attribute protection gaps to legal deficits, weak implementation, or failures of coordination. These factors matter. But they do not explain why fragmentation persists within functioning institutions.

This research approaches fragmentation as a problem of institutional design. Governance systems organised around fixed categories, bounded eligibility, and territorial mandates produce fragmentation as a predictable, self-reinforcing outcome — not simply as a failure of political will or technical capacity.

The same systems that routinely extend sophisticated coverage to documented workers, taxpayers, and citizens exclude displaced populations because their presence cannot be defined, bounded, or closed in advance.

Membership Regimes

Who belongs

Legal categories define the boundaries of political community. Governance systems for labour migration, social protection, and civil registration are built around populations whose membership can be defined, bounded, and reversed. Displacement unsettles all three conditions.

Fiscal Containment

Which obligations become permanent

Budget architecture embeds mechanisms that prevent exceptional expenditure from converting into permanent obligation. Emergency allocations expire. Registries require requalification. Parallel structures extend coverage without shifting mandate authority or budget lines.

Mandate Architecture

Where responsibility begins and ends

Ministries derive authority from sector-specific roles that define their fiscal accountability. Absorbing displaced populations requires mandate expansion, which is institutionally costly regardless of the populations’ vulnerabilities.

Applications

Across displacement contexts

The frameworks are applied across the full range of displacement and mobility contexts. A common diagnostic question runs through each: how can public systems be designed to follow people through displacement rather than requiring people to fit the categories of institutions?

Refugee Governance and Asylum Systems

When legal recognition determines access to public systems

The research examines how legal recognition thresholds interact with fiscal and administrative exclusion, particularly in non-signatory states.

Internal Displacement

When national responsibility lacks institutional continuity

Internal displacement exposes how responsibility can remain formally domestic while becoming fragmented across ministries, territories, and phases of response.

Development-Induced Displacement

When project safeguards end before vulnerability does

Development-induced displacement illustrates the orphan condition of project-affected communities and the limits of safeguard frameworks that do not connect people to national systems.

Adaptive Social Protection

When eligibility systems cannot follow mobility

Social protection architecture must be able to follow people across displacement types, beyond climate and disaster triggers alone.

Disaster and Climate Displacement

When emergency systems confront longitudinal vulnerability

Disaster and climate displacement reveal temporal fault lines in public systems designed for emergency response rather than recurrent vulnerability.

Development Finance and Infrastructure

When financing architecture shapes governance outcomes

IFI–sovereign engagement offers a reform entry point for connecting project obligations, public finance, and national absorption capacity.

Research Question

How can public systems be designed to follow people through displacement — rather than requiring people to fit the boundaries of institutions?

Displacement Policy approaches governance and protection as institutional design problems, with financing architecture as a primary entry point for reform.
