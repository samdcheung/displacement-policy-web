Research Agenda

Displacement Governance and Public Systems

Research on how institutions, public systems and financing arrangements sustain responsibility across displacement.

Research Agenda

How can responsibility for displaced populations be assigned, financed, transferred and sustained across institutions and over time?

Displacement is commonly approached through legal categories, humanitarian mandates and sector-specific responses. Yet people experience displacement differently: across time, territory, legal status and institutions.

Public systems are organised around fixed eligibility rules, administrative boundaries, institutional mandates and fiscal arrangements. These systems rarely move with people.

The result is institutional discontinuity. Responsibility, eligibility, services, data and financing can weaken or end as displaced populations move across jurisdictions, programmes and phases of displacement.

Displacement Policy examines these fault lines — how institutional design, political economy and public finance shape where responsibility begins, where it fragments, and whether it can be sustained over time.

Governance Framework

A Governance Framework for Displacement

The research agenda is organised around three connected ideas: the displacement continuum, sustainable inclusion and political economy.

Displacement Continuum

Diagnostic: where responsibility fragments across category, territory and time.

Sustainable Inclusion

Direction of reform: public-system inclusion, regular financing and continuing institutional responsibility.

Political Economy

Feasibility: membership, mandates, fiscal systems and incentives.

The Displacement Continuum

The displacement continuum is a governance diagnostic.

Displacement rarely occurs as a single event or within a stable category. People move across legal statuses, jurisdictions, institutions and phases of vulnerability. Governance systems, by contrast, are organised around fixed categories and bounded mandates.

The result is a series of fault lines where responsibility does not transfer with people.

These are often:

Categorical — when eligibility changes with legal or administrative status.

Territorial — when people move but authority and financing remain tied to place.

Temporal — when emergency programmes, project cycles or temporary funding end before displacement-related needs do.

The continuum therefore shifts attention from which framework formally applies to whether responsibility remains continuous as people move through different systems.

Fragmentation is not simply a coordination problem. It is often produced by institutional design.

Sustainable Inclusion

Sustainable inclusion describes the direction of reform.

It asks how displacement-related risks and responsibilities can be embedded within ordinary institutions and financing arrangements rather than remaining dependent on exceptional or temporary structures.

It does not imply one model of legal incorporation. Institutional arrangements must be politically feasible, administratively workable and fiscally sustainable.

Three conditions matter:

Public-system inclusion

Protection and essential services are connected, where possible, to ordinary systems such as social protection, health, education, civil registration and local government.

Regular financing

Displacement-related costs are recognised through public budgets, intergovernmental transfers, development finance or other predictable financing arrangements.

Continuing institutional responsibility

Mandates remain clear as people move between jurisdictions, programmes and phases of displacement.

The central test is whether public systems can absorb displacement-related vulnerability while sustaining responsibility over time.

Political Economy

Institutional fragmentation is not always accidental.

Existing arrangements may persist because they limit fiscal exposure, preserve institutional mandates, maintain sovereign discretion or reflect wider political interests.

Three dynamics recur.

Membership determines who is recognised and eligible.

Mandates determine where institutional responsibility begins and ends.

Fiscal systems determine whether temporary expenditure can become continuing public obligation.

Political economy therefore asks not only what reform is needed, but why existing arrangements persist, where incentives for change exist and what forms of reform may be feasible.

This matters particularly where comprehensive legal or institutional reform is politically constrained.

In such contexts, responsibility may still be strengthened through sectoral systems, administrative adaptation, fiscal arrangements, development finance or other institutional entry points.

Areas of Inquiry

Four connected areas of research

The framework is applied across four connected areas of research.

Public Systems and Social Protection

Displaced populations often lose access to protection and essential services when they move across districts, registries, legal categories or programme phases.

This area examines whether public systems can respond not only to shocks, but to mobility.

Research focuses on:

  • portability of benefits and eligibility;
  • interoperable registries and identification systems;
  • continuity across jurisdictions;
  • humanitarian-public system interfaces;
  • duration-sensitive support; and
  • financing arrangements that follow populations as they move.

A particular focus is the distinction between vertical scalability — expanding support during a shock — and horizontal integration — maintaining responsibility as people move across systems and jurisdictions.

Engagement fields: social protection, humanitarian policy, health and education systems, civil registration, digital public infrastructure, public administration and service delivery.

Development, Infrastructure and Displacement

Development-induced displacement exposes a wider governance problem: responsibility is often concentrated at the moment of project impact, while displacement-related consequences continue long after project closure.

This area examines how infrastructure finance, safeguards and development-bank operations can connect project-based responses with longer-term public responsibility.

Research focuses on:

  • institutional responsibility after project closure;
  • links between resettlement and public systems;
  • fiscal arrangements for receiving jurisdictions;
  • the limits of project-based safeguards; and
  • how infrastructure finance and country programming can support longer-term governance reform.

The broader question is how to move from project compliance to governance design.

Engagement fields: multilateral development banks, infrastructure finance, environmental and social safeguards, development-induced displacement, public investment, local government and ESG frameworks.

Climate, Disasters and Mobility

Climate and disaster systems are increasingly able to respond to shocks. The harder question is whether they can sustain responsibility when people move.

This area examines how adaptive social protection, disaster risk management, anticipatory action, planned mobility and resilience finance function across the displacement continuum.

Research focuses on:

  • continuity after movement;
  • portability of services and entitlements;
  • financing for receiving jurisdictions;
  • transitions from emergency response to longer-term support;
  • planned relocation and public-system inclusion; and
  • the role of adaptation, resilience and Loss and Damage financing in supporting longer-term absorption.

The focus is not simply on climate mobility, but on the institutional consequences of mobility.

Engagement fields: climate mobility, disaster risk reduction, adaptation, resilience, anticipatory action, planned relocation, climate finance and Loss and Damage.

Legal Status, Territory and Multilevel Governance

Displacement often crosses the boundaries through which responsibility is legally and administratively organised.

This area examines what happens when people move between legal statuses, jurisdictions, national systems and regional frameworks.

Research focuses on:

  • responsibility across legal and administrative categories;
  • national-local institutional arrangements;
  • documentation and eligibility across jurisdictions;
  • regional cooperation;
  • institutional handoffs; and
  • the relationship between legal protection and administrative systems.

The aim is not to replace legal analysis, but to examine how legal protection is translated — or fails to translate — into institutional continuity.

Engagement fields: refugee and IDP protection, migration governance, local government, regional institutions, public administration and legal policy.

Cross-Cutting Strand

Financing Displacement

Public finance is a major cross-cutting strand of the research agenda.

Displacement is usually financed as an exceptional event even when its consequences become long-term. Humanitarian funding, project finance, disaster instruments and public budgets operate through different rules and time horizons, often reproducing the same institutional discontinuities found elsewhere in displacement governance.

Displacement Policy examines how these financing architectures shape what governments are willing and able to sustain — from public expenditure and intergovernmental finance to development finance, sovereign risk and capital markets.

The research asks how financing can support institutional inclusion without converting displacement into an undefined fiscal liability, and whether displacement-related investments can increasingly be understood as part of resilience, public-system capacity and long-term risk management.

This strand connects current work on the fiscal architecture of displacement with emerging research on sovereign finance and capital markets.

Cross-Cutting Lenses

Lenses that run across the research agenda

Institutional Design

Where is responsibility located, where does it end, and what happens when people cross institutional boundaries?

Political Economy

Why do fragmented arrangements persist, whose interests do they protect, and under what conditions can reform occur?

Public Finance and Financing Architecture

Displacement creates costs that routinely outlast humanitarian appeals, emergency budgets and project cycles. Yet the fiscal systems that ultimately determine whether states can sustain these costs are rarely designed around displacement.

Research examines how displacement interacts with:

  • public budgets and medium-term expenditure;
  • intergovernmental fiscal transfers;
  • contingent and recurrent liabilities;
  • disaster and resilience financing;
  • development-bank financing;
  • sovereign borrowing and debt sustainability; and
  • the incentives created by sovereign risk and capital markets.

The central question is how displacement-related expenditure can move from temporary and exceptional financing toward fiscally credible arrangements for longer-term public responsibility — without creating perceptions of unlimited or open-ended liability.

Data, Identity and Interoperability

Can people remain visible to institutions as they move across programmes, jurisdictions and legal categories?

Leadership and Institutional Change

How do decision-makers navigate competing mandates, fiscal constraints and political incentives to sustain protection outcomes over time?

Reform Pathways

Reform at several points in the governance architecture

Displacement Policy examines reform at several points in the governance architecture.

These include:

  • administrative systems — eligibility, documentation, registries and portability;
  • institutional mandates — responsibility allocation and handoffs;
  • public systems — social protection, health, education, civil registration and local government;
  • public finance — budgets, intergovernmental transfers and fiscal risk;
  • development finance — infrastructure, country programming, safeguards and resilience finance; and
  • regional and legal frameworks — arrangements that shape responsibility across jurisdictions.

The most viable reform pathway will differ by context.

Where comprehensive legal reform is constrained, progress may still be possible through institutional, sectoral or fiscal entry points.

From Research to Policy Application

Selective applied work where research identifies a credible pathway for reform

Displacement Policy is primarily an independent research platform.

Applied work develops selectively where research identifies a clear institutional problem and a credible pathway for reform.

The approach is straightforward.

Diagnose the fault line

Where does responsibility, eligibility, financing or service continuity break down?

Understand the political economy

Why does the gap persist, who bears the cost, and what incentives shape reform?

Identify the entry point

Does the most viable pathway lie in administrative systems, public services, institutional mandates, fiscal architecture, development finance or legal reform?

Develop the reform pathway

What institutional, fiscal or policy changes could realistically improve continuity over time?

Applied work may include country diagnostics, institutional assessments, comparative research, fiscal and financing analysis, reform options and implementation roadmaps.

The purpose is not to prescribe a universal model.

It is to identify where responsibility breaks down, why it does so, and what forms of institutional change may realistically improve continuity.

Closing Proposition

Designing institutions and public systems that sustain responsibility across displacement

Displacement governance is often organised around the event that caused movement or the legal category assigned afterward.

Displaced people experience something different: a longitudinal condition in which vulnerability, protection and institutional responsibility evolve across time, territory and systems.

The central challenge is therefore not simply how to respond to displacement.

It is how to design institutions and public systems that sustain responsibility across it.
