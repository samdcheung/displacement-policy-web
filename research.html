Research Agenda

Research Agenda: Displacement Governance and Public Systems

Research on how public systems carry responsibility through displacement.

How responsibility for displaced populations is assigned, financed, and sustained across public systems

Displacement is commonly treated as a humanitarian challenge. Yet access to protection, services, and longer-term solutions ultimately depends on public systems: the institutions governing legal identity, welfare, health, education, territory, infrastructure, local administration, and public finance.

Displacement Policy examines why displaced populations fall outside these systems — not only because institutions or legal protections are absent, but because existing systems are organised around categories, jurisdictions, mandates, and fiscal rules that do not follow people through displacement.

The research agenda asks how responsibility for displaced populations can be more clearly assigned, financed, transferred, and sustained across institutions and over time.

The Central Question

Where does institutional responsibility break down?

People move across legal statuses, administrative boundaries, service systems, and phases of vulnerability. Public institutions, by contrast, are generally organised around fixed eligibility rules, territorial authority, bounded mandates, project periods, and annual budget cycles.

Responsibility therefore often becomes fragmented or ends at transition points:

  • when emergency assistance ends;
  • when people cross a district or national boundary;
  • when legal or administrative status changes;
  • when a project closes before livelihoods are restored;
  • when responsibility shifts between humanitarian, development, national, and local institutions.

The result is a recurring gap between how displacement is lived and how governance is organised.

This agenda treats those gaps not simply as coordination failures, but as problems of institutional design, financing, and political economy.

Analytical Framework

The research is organised around three connected lenses.

The Displacement Continuum

Tracks where responsibility becomes fragmented as people move across status, territory, institutions, and time.

Sustainable Inclusion

Defines the conditions required for ordinary public systems to maintain responsibility over time.

Political Economy

Explains how rules, mandates, fiscal systems, and incentives shape inclusion, exclusion, and continuity.

The Displacement Continuum

The Displacement Continuum identifies where responsibility becomes fragmented as people move across categories, territories, institutions, and time.

It shifts attention from displacement as a single event or fixed legal status to the sequence of institutional systems people encounter throughout displacement.

The framework asks:

  • which institution is responsible at each stage;
  • when and why that responsibility ends;
  • whether eligibility, data, and financing transfer across systems;
  • what happens when no institution is required to assume responsibility next.

The continuum is therefore used as a governance diagnostic: a way of identifying the categorical, territorial, temporal, administrative, and fiscal boundaries at which protection and access to services break down.

Sustainable Inclusion

Sustainable Inclusion asks what is required for ordinary public systems to maintain responsibility for displacement-related needs over time.

It does not mean the immediate or unconditional incorporation of every displaced population into every national programme. It refers instead to the institutional arrangements through which displaced people can access protection and essential services on terms that are administratively workable, fiscally credible, and politically sustainable.

The framework focuses on three conditions.

Integration into public systems.
Displacement-related needs are addressed through ordinary institutions — including health, education, social protection, civil registration, housing, and local government — rather than exclusively through parallel humanitarian structures.

Integration into regular financing.
The costs associated with displacement are recognised within public budgets, intergovernmental transfers, development finance, contingency mechanisms, or other predictable financing arrangements.

Clear and continuing institutional responsibility.
Mandates identify which institutions remain responsible across emergency, recovery, integration, return, relocation, and other transitions.

Political Economy

Institutional fragmentation is not always accidental.

Membership rules determine who belongs. Mandates determine where authority begins and ends. Fiscal systems limit the conversion of temporary expenditure into continuing obligation. Political incentives shape which populations governments are prepared to recognise, finance, and include.

The agenda therefore examines not only what institutional reform requires, but also the conditions under which governments and public systems may be willing and able to undertake it.

This includes attention to:

  • sovereignty and territorial control;
  • citizenship and population membership;
  • mandate protection;
  • fiscal risk and liability;
  • national and local political incentives;
  • donor financing and international influence;
  • the distribution of costs between institutions and levels of government.

Governance Mapping

Building the comparative evidence base

The Southeast Asia Displacement Governance Mapping is the principal evidence-building component of the research agenda.

It examines how displacement is governed through laws, institutions, registries, public systems, safeguards, and financing arrangements across the region.

The mapping identifies:

  • where responsibility is formally assigned;
  • which institutions exercise authority;
  • whether displaced populations are connected to ordinary public systems;
  • whether eligibility, documentation, data, and financing remain valid when people move;
  • where responsibility becomes fragmented across categories, sectors, territories, and time;
  • which recurring governance patterns appear across countries and institutions.

Entries are organised by country, governance layer, sector, and political-economy archetype. They bring together national policies, regional instruments, administrative systems, financing arrangements, and institutional practices that are often examined separately.

The current focus is Southeast Asia, while also including regional and global instruments where they shape national governance.

The mapping is intended to support comparison across countries and systems, identify recurring fault lines, and generate questions for policy briefs, working papers, and future applied research.

Current Areas of Work

Current research priorities

The research programme currently concentrates on four areas. Across each, the agenda examines how responsibility is assigned between institutions, how access is maintained as people move, and how continuing obligations are financed.

Public Systems and Social Protection

Displaced populations frequently lose access to protection and essential services when they move across districts, registries, legal categories, or programme phases.

Social protection systems are becoming more capable of expanding during crises. They are less consistently designed to maintain eligibility, data, financing, and institutional responsibility after the immediate emergency or across administrative boundaries.

This area examines:

  • the portability of benefits and eligibility;
  • connections between humanitarian registration and national systems;
  • responsibility between sending and receiving jurisdictions;
  • transitions from emergency assistance to sustained protection;
  • access to health, education, civil registration, housing, and other public services;
  • financing arrangements that allow public systems to respond without weakening provision for existing populations.

The central question is not only whether displaced populations can be included, but whether public systems can preserve access and responsibility through movement and over time.

Development, Infrastructure, and Displacement

Infrastructure, urban development, conservation, and other public or private investments can generate displacement while also creating formal obligations for compensation, resettlement, and livelihood restoration.

Those obligations often remain bounded by project timelines. When implementation ends, affected populations may be left without clear access to ordinary services, local institutions, or continuing livelihood support.

This area examines:

  • the relationship between project safeguards and public systems;
  • institutional responsibility after project closure;
  • the capacity of local government to support resettled populations;
  • the treatment of displacement risk in project appraisal and financing;
  • the division of responsibility between governments, development banks, contractors, and investors;
  • whether public and private finance adequately account for the longer-term consequences of displacement.

The research asks how development institutions can move beyond procedural compliance toward arrangements that remain effective after a project's formal obligations end.

Climate, Disasters, and Mobility

Disaster and climate policies are commonly organised around identifiable events, affected locations, and defined emergency periods. Displacement may instead become repeated, protracted, or permanent.

Households can move between evacuation, temporary shelter, relocation, informal settlement, and secondary migration without any institution remaining clearly responsible across the full trajectory.

This area examines:

  • connections between anticipatory action, emergency response, recovery, and longer-term public systems;
  • disaster registration and its relationship to social protection and civil-registration systems;
  • planned relocation and the governance of receiving communities;
  • local government responsibility for mobile and displaced populations;
  • the treatment of displacement within climate adaptation and public investment;
  • the transition from emergency finance to medium-term fiscal and development planning.

The research considers how disaster-risk and climate systems can address displacement as a continuing governance condition rather than only as the immediate consequence of an event.

Legal Status, Territory, and Multilevel Governance

Responsibility for displaced populations is divided across legal categories, territorial jurisdictions, sectors, and levels of government.

Refugees, internally displaced people, migrants, returnees, stateless populations, and disaster-displaced communities may face similar institutional barriers while being governed under different legal and administrative frameworks. Comprehensive status reform may also be politically constrained.

This area examines:

  • how legal and administrative categories determine access to public institutions;
  • where sector-specific inclusion is possible without comprehensive status reform;
  • how responsibility changes when people cross borders or administrative boundaries;
  • the role of documentation, civil registration, and identity systems;
  • the distribution of mandates and financing between central and local government;
  • the influence of regional institutions, soft-law frameworks, and cross-border arrangements.

The research asks how responsibility can be made clearer and more continuous even where legal status, sovereignty, and territorial authority remain contested.

Research Outputs

How the agenda is advanced

The agenda is advanced through publications, comparative research, expert perspectives, and institutional engagement.

Policy Briefs

The Governing Displacement brief series applies the research framework to specific institutional problems.

Each brief identifies a governance gap, examines the mandates, systems, and financing arrangements that produce it, and proposes practical directions for reform.

Current and planned themes include:

  • development-induced displacement and infrastructure safeguards;
  • adaptive social protection and the portability of protection;
  • the fiscal architecture of displacement;
  • financing inclusion and sovereign resilience;
  • the relationship between displacement risk and development finance.

The briefs are designed for policy practitioners, public institutions, development banks, humanitarian organisations, and researchers seeking concise analysis of a defined governance problem.

Working Papers and Longer Research

Working papers develop the agenda's conceptual, comparative, and empirical foundations in greater depth.

They examine how public systems, development finance, legal frameworks, and governance arrangements distribute responsibility for displacement.

They also test propositions emerging from the Governance Mapping and policy briefs, including:

  • how institutional discontinuity is produced;
  • how responsibility is transferred or lost between systems;
  • how public finance shapes the limits of inclusion;
  • how political incentives condition reform;
  • how governance practices compare across displacement contexts.

Policy Perspectives

The Perspectives series brings external expertise and institutional experience into the research agenda.

Contributors examine what a shift from humanitarian response to displacement governance means for protection, public institutions, financing, leadership, legal frameworks, and institutional reform.

Perspectives are not intended to present a single institutional position. They are designed to:

  • test and challenge the research agenda;
  • bring practical experience into the analysis;
  • identify unresolved policy questions;
  • introduce competing interpretations;
  • surface areas requiring further research.

Applied Analysis and Institutional Dialogue

The agenda is also developed through case analysis, expert dialogue, and engagement with governments, humanitarian organisations, development banks, researchers, civil society, and public-system specialists.

This work examines whether the agenda's frameworks can support practical reform in areas such as:

  • institutional mandates;
  • programme eligibility;
  • social registries and data systems;
  • public-service delivery;
  • infrastructure safeguards;
  • budgeting and intergovernmental finance;
  • contingency and development financing;
  • accountability across institutional transitions.

Applied engagement also helps identify where conceptual arguments require further evidence, qualification, or adaptation to different political and institutional settings.

Intended Contribution

A cumulative body of research

Displacement Policy aims to produce a cumulative body of research rather than a collection of disconnected publications.

The Governance Mapping provides the comparative evidence base.

Policy briefs isolate concrete institutional and financing problems.

Working papers develop and test the wider conceptual argument.

Perspectives bring external expertise, institutional experience, and critical challenge into the programme.

Applied analysis examines whether the resulting propositions can support practical institutional reform.

Together, these outputs seek to identify where responsibility for displaced populations becomes fragmented, unassigned, or unsustainable — and how it can be more clearly allocated, financed, transferred, and maintained over time.

The wider objective is to move displacement policy beyond the assumption that humanitarian response alone can resolve failures rooted in public systems.

Displacement becomes governable over time only when institutions are designed to preserve responsibility across changes in status, territory, programme, and phase.
