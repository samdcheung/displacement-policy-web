Research

Why capable states fail displaced populations — and what governance design can do about it.

Research examining how responsibility for displaced populations is organised, financed, and sustained across institutions, public systems, and territories.

Much displacement policy remains organised around legal categories, humanitarian response, and emergency financing. This research explores a different question: how states absorb displacement through ordinary systems of governance.

The programme argues that displacement governance often fails not because states lack capacity, but because fragmentation is built into institutional design — serving fiscal containment, membership governance, and mandate protection simultaneously.

The Displacement Continuum

Three fault lines in institutional design.

Categorical

Status without continuity

Eligibility systems organised around categories such as citizen, worker, irregular migrant, refugee, or disaster-affected person often reset responsibility even when vulnerability continues.

Temporal

Emergency design for chronic reality

Budget authority is annual, emergency declarations expire, and programme eligibility requires requalification, even when displacement becomes prolonged.

Territorial

Jurisdictions that do not follow people

Social registries, civil registration, and service delivery systems are administered by fixed units, while displaced populations move across administrative boundaries.

Named Findings

Concepts developed through the research programme.

Brief 1 / Chapter

Paradox of Capacity

States with sophisticated administrative systems may still produce fragmented outcomes for displaced populations because institutions are designed around fixed categories and bounded mandates.

Brief 1

Sustainable Inclusion

Embedding displacement-related vulnerability within ordinary public systems including health, education, social protection, civil registration, and local administration.

Brief 2

Infrastructure Finance Gap

Development-induced displacement often extends beyond project safeguards, leaving affected populations governed at the point of displacement but institutionally orphaned afterward.

Brief 3

Precision Exclusion Problem

Adaptive social protection systems are increasingly precise; when they exclude forcibly displaced people, exclusion may be architectural rather than accidental.

Brief 4

Recurrent Fiscal Obligation Problem

Sovereign fiscal systems resist treating displacement-related spending as a recurrent obligation, even when displacement creates long-term pressure on public systems.

Brief 5

Displacement as Sovereign Risk

Displacement-responsive systems can reduce future instability and emergency expenditure, but are often treated as fiscal liabilities rather than resilience investments.

Featured Analysis

Two frameworks that underpin the broader research agenda.

Governance Framework

The Displacement Continuum

Protection failures often occur where governance systems stop following people across categorical, temporal, and territorial boundaries.

The continuum provides a diagnostic for identifying these fault lines and understanding fragmentation as a consequence of institutional design.

Fiscal Framework

Fiscal Architecture of Displacement

Long-term displacement is ultimately absorbed through budgets, social protection systems, infrastructure investment, and sovereign financing arrangements.

This framework examines how public finance shapes state absorption capacity and long-term inclusion.

Regional Application

Southeast Asia serves as the first regional application of the research programme.

The region combines sophisticated migration systems, social protection programmes, disaster-risk institutions, and development finance frameworks, yet persistent displacement governance gaps remain.

The Southeast Asia Displacement Governance Mapping project applies these frameworks to understand where governance systems break down and where institutional absorption remains possible.
