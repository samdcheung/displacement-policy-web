Mapping

A structured view of responsibility, systems, and fault lines.

This mapping examines governance, institutional responsibility, and fiscal authority across multiple displacement-related sectors. It shows where responsibility is assigned, where public systems absorb displacement-related vulnerability, and where governance breaks across categories, territories, and time.

The purpose is not to score countries. It is to make visible the institutional fault lines that determine whether displaced populations remain connected to ordinary systems after status, location, crisis phase, or project boundaries change.

How to use it

Start with the overall diagnostics, then use the country selector, governance layers, fault-line view, or illustrative cases to examine how responsibility is assigned and where continuity breaks.

Institutional continuity is the central test.

The mapping shows that displacement governance turns less on whether a country has a dedicated displacement framework and more on whether ordinary systems continue to carry responsibility after status, location, crisis phase, or project boundaries change.

Not a ranking. Not a scorecard. The dashboard identifies recurring governance patterns and where responsibility is most likely to break.

Absorption

Functional access can expand without status reform.

Health, social protection, and local delivery systems may absorb vulnerability when public-system incentives align.

Continuity

Emergency activation does not equal long-term responsibility.

Response capacity often weakens when people move into recovery, relocation, or ordinary welfare systems.

Data

Legibility is not the same as portability.

Registries can make people visible to the state while still failing to transfer support across territory or status.

Finance

Project and budget boundaries shape protection.

Compensation, safeguards, and emergency funds rarely create durable fiscal responsibility after the bounded intervention ends.
